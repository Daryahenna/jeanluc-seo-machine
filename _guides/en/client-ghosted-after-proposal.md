Last May I sent a proposal for €38,000: a year-long consulting engagement for a logistics company.

On the call the client was on fire: “That’s exactly what we need. Send the deck.”

I sent it.

Silence.

After a week I wrote the standard “Did you have a chance to look?” Silence.

After two: “I understand you’re busy… whenever you have a minute…” Silence.

Three months later I learned from the news they signed with a competitor.

For **less money, but a week before their deadline**.

I reviewed it with my coach and he said one line that only landed a month later:

**“Silence isn’t ‘the client is thinking’. It’s ‘the client already chose, and usually not you.’”**

Then I built a system: 5 touches over 21 days.

Now, about a third of “ghosted” deals come back.

Not magic. I just stopped waiting for weather to change.

<div class="callout is-key">
<p class="callout-title">⚡️ Key idea</p>
<p>Silence after a proposal isn’t a pause. It’s a <strong>race against a competitor</strong>. While you wait politely, someone else is having coffee with your client, sending cases, staying top of mind. Your job isn’t “not to annoy”. It’s <strong>not to let the client forget</strong>. Those sound similar. They’re not.</p>
</div>

## Why clients go silent after a proposal

Golden Key rule: if the client disappeared, it means you **didn’t lock the next step** in the meeting.

You gave them “freedom to disappear”.

No date. No goal. No commitment.

In reality, “ghosted” usually means one of five situations:

<ul class="check">
<li><strong>They opened it, postponed it, forgot</strong> — classic B2B. They meant to read it “in the evening” and never came back. Not angry, not a no — just absorbed by fires.</li>
<li><strong>They’re talking to competitors</strong> — collecting 2-3 proposals to compare. They don’t reply because they haven’t chosen and don’t want to “give hope”.</li>
<li><strong>An internal blocker appeared</strong> — CFO said “not now”, partner is against it, situation changed. They don’t write because saying “no” feels uncomfortable.</li>
<li><strong>They didn’t understand the proposal</strong> — too complex, no clear price/path. Asking feels awkward, so they avoid it.</li>
<li><strong>It’s not a priority anymore</strong> — on the call it was “wow”, a week later it’s “something about marketing, I need to figure it out”. It sinks.</li>
</ul>

**In 4 out of 5 cases it’s not a no.**

It’s a pause you can turn into “yes” if you act correctly.

And in 1 out of 5 it’s a real “no” — but a legitimate, soft “no” you still want to extract so the deal doesn’t hang in your pipeline for half a year.

<div class="callout is-tip">
<p class="callout-title">💡 Rule #1</p>
<p>Never end a meeting/call/chat without <strong>two calendar points</strong>: the proposal send date and the return date. “I’ll send by Tuesday, we’ll talk Thursday at 11 just to confirm it’s clear.” Without that you’re not “in process”. You’re waiting for a miracle.</p>
</div>

## What NOT to write after a proposal

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“Hello! Did you have a chance to look at our proposal? Best, [name]”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? **It’s an empty touch.**

No reason. No value. No new information.

The client reads it in 2 seconds and postpones: “I’ll answer when I can” (never).

Worse: it signals “I’m nervous and need confirmation”, which weakens your position.

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“I know you’re very busy and I don’t want to be pushy… just checking if our offer is still relevant?”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? **You apologized for your outreach.**

“I don’t want to be pushy” makes them think: “So you are pushy.”

Also “still relevant?” is easy to answer with “not really” or ignore.

You offered them the exit.

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“Reminder: our offer is only valid until the end of the week. If you don’t decide, we’ll have to re-quote.”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? **Fake deadline.**

They feel it, especially if you never mentioned any timeline before.

Instead of speeding up the deal, you speed up their exit.

<div class="callout is-quote">
<p class="callout-title">Quote</p>
<p>“While you stay silent for six months ‘to be polite’, your competitor has coffee with your client once a month, sends industry reports, makes intros. When the client decides, they decide with the person who stayed close.”</p>
<cite>— bitslammer, r/sales (based on a 154+ upvote observation)</cite>
</div>

## Step 1. 24 hours after sending: secure the next micro-step

**Goal:** confirm they opened it and lock a micro-step.

Not “thoughts?”, but “clarity check”.

<div class="phrase-card">“Hey! I sent the proposal yesterday. When you sit down to review it, I can jump on for 10 minutes for any questions so you don’t have to guess from the text. Better Tuesday before noon or Wednesday after 2pm?”</div>

Why it works:
1. Not “Did you read it?”, but “When will you review it?”
2. You offer help, not a demand
3. You include a choice-question (two concrete slots)
4. You remove the fear of “figuring it out from a PDF”

In ~60% of cases you’ll either get a call booked or a short reply like “Thanks, I’ll review Wednesday and get back to you.” In both cases, you’re in their calendar.

## Step 2. Day 3: touch with a reason

**Goal:** not “remind them you exist”, but **make the decision easier**.

Bring something useful that connects to the proposal: a case, a small calculation, an answer to a likely question.

<div class="phrase-card">“[Name], while you’re reviewing the proposal, I’m sending a short 1-page case from a company in your space. They started in March in a similar situation and by August hit [specific number]. If you want, we can discuss what’s transferable to you.”</div>

Three good reasons for touch #2:

<ul class="check">
<li><strong>A case study from an adjacent industry</strong> — specific before/after numbers, no fluff</li>
<li><strong>A useful resource for their pain</strong> — article, research, checklist (yours or external)</li>
<li><strong>An answer to a likely question</strong> — “I thought you might be wondering about X. Here’s a short answer.”</li>
</ul>

**Don’t offer discounts at this stage.**

It signals panic. Your price should stay firm at least until day 14.

## Step 3. Day 7: direct question about the process

**Goal:** understand where the decision is.

Not “what did you decide?”, but “how is it moving?”.

<div class="phrase-card">“[Name], how is the proposal discussion going? So I can plan — should we try to fit into your schedule, or should I step back and come back in a month?”</div>

This is strong because you say what most people hide: the option to step back.

The client feels you’re helping them be honest.

Possible responses and what you do next:

### Scenario A: “We’re discussing it, we need a week”

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Script:</strong> “Got it. Then I’ll send one short message next week on Wednesday — no pressure. If you’re ready, we’ll talk. If it’s still early, just say ‘one more week’ and I won’t chase. Works?”</p>
</div>

### Scenario B: “We have an internal blocker”

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Script:</strong> “Okay, understood. What kind of blocker is it — finance, approval, priorities? I might be able to help: write a short forwardable text, or adjust the format so it gets past the blocker. If we can’t, we’ll let it go with no hard feelings.”</p>
</div>

Often one focused step removes the blocker. See ["I need to consult with someone"]({{ site.baseurl }}/en/guides/objection-need-to-consult/) for the exact mechanics.

### Scenario C: “We’re also looking at competitors”

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Script:</strong> “Makes sense. What parameters are you comparing — price, scope, timelines, accountability? I can build a quick comparison table across those axes so you don’t have to. No propaganda, just facts.”</p>
</div>

You need to know what they’re comparing you against. It matters for day 14.

### Scenario D: “Not now / not a priority”

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Script:</strong> “Got it, thanks for being honest. Then I won’t message for the next month. In a month I’ll send one short note — two lines on what’s new. If it becomes relevant, we’ll book. If not, just say ‘not now’ and I’ll step back. Fair?”</p>
</div>

Don’t push. Lock a return point and let it go. About 25-30% come back in 2-6 months.

## Steps 4 and 5. Days 14 and 21: narrow and release

**Day 14.** If after day 7 you still have no clear answer, this is your last “sales” touch.

Not a reminder. A concrete micro-step proposal.

<div class="phrase-card">“[Name], three options for you: (1) a 15-minute call to review the proposal this week; (2) if you have one specific question, send it and I’ll answer within 30 minutes; (3) if it’s out of priority, just say ‘not now’ and I’ll step back for 2 months. Which is best?”</p></div>

Three options, including an exit, reduces tension and (paradoxically) increases reply rate 2-3x.

It’s easier to pick 1 of 3 than to write an explanation.

**Day 21. Final release message.** If after day 14 it’s still silence:

<div class="phrase-card">“Okay, I’m reading this as the topic isn’t heating up right now. No hard feelings: I’m closing the loop until you ping me. If anything changes, message me and I’ll reply within 10 minutes. Until then, I won’t chase.”</div>

This is not passive-aggressive.

It’s clean loop closure.

In ~80% of cases it unlocks a reply within 24 hours: “Sorry, I was drowning. Let’s talk Wednesday.” In 20% it stays silent, and that’s real “not now”.

## Real chat dialog

Resurrecting a €22,000 deal for a sales team training package.

The client went silent for 11 days after the proposal.

This is exactly what I wrote in Telegram.

<div class="dialog">
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">Hey! I sent the proposal on the 16th. If you’re reviewing it this week, I have 10 minutes Friday before noon for any questions so you don’t have to decode it from the text.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">Hey, sorry — got buried in the quarterly report. I’ll look over the weekend.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">All good. Just in case, so you don’t have to “guess from a PDF” — I’m sending a short 1-page case on how a similar company (B2B, 80 people) increased cold-call conversion from 8% to 19% in 90 days. Might help your decision.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">(silence for 5 days)</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">[Name], how is the proposal discussion going? So I can plan — do we still have a shot to fit into your schedule, or should I step back for a month?</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">Honestly, our CFO says “Q3 is heavy, let’s do Q4”. I’m undecided.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">Got it. One question: is he blocked by the amount, or by timing (“not right now”)? That changes how I can help — for example, split payments into 3 tranches, or write him a short forwardable note on ROI.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">More timing. Amount is fine, he just doesn’t want to pull cash right now.</span></p>

On Friday: yes. No discount, no pressure.

The deal simply stopped hanging.

## 8 ready-to-use follow-up lines

<div class="phrase-card">“I sent the proposal yesterday. When you review it, I have 10 minutes for any questions. Tuesday before noon or Wednesday after 2pm?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Sending a short case from your space — one page. Might help as you discuss it.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“How is the proposal discussion going? So I can plan — should I stay close or step back for a month?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“What parameters are you comparing? I can build a quick comparison table — facts only.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“What kind of blocker is it — finance, approval, priorities? That tells me how I can help.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Three options: (1) 15-minute call, (2) one question by text, (3) ‘not now’ — I step back for 2 months. Which is best?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Okay, not heating up. I’m closing the loop until you ping me. If anything changes — message me.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“We start [date], pay in three tranches. Does that payment format fit?”</div>

## Checklist before you send a follow-up

<ul class="check">
<li>Does this message have a real reason (case / resource / question)?</li>
<li>Am I avoiding “I know you’re busy” / “I don’t want to bother you”?</li>
<li>Is there a micro-step (pick a slot, yes/no, one concrete action)?</li>
<li>Am I avoiding discounting as my first move?</li>
<li>Do I know what I’ll do if there’s no reply for 7 days?</li>
</ul>

If you have one “no”, rewrite it.

## Common mistakes

<div class="callout is-warn">
<p class="callout-title">⚠️ What kills follow-up</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Empty “did you have a chance” touches.</strong> No reason = no read.</li>
<li><strong>Apologizing for outreach.</strong> “Don’t want to bother” confirms you’re bothering.</li>
<li><strong>Discounting on the 2nd touch.</strong> Signals panic, devalues price.</li>
<li><strong>Switching to a colder channel.</strong> Chat → stay in chat.</li>
<li><strong>Fake deadlines.</strong> “Valid until Friday” without a real reason destroys trust.</li>
<li><strong>Pinging every 2 days.</strong> Third “any update?” in a week gets you muted.</li>
<li><strong>Never finalizing.</strong> If you don’t release cleanly, the deal hangs for months and ruins your pipeline analytics.</li>
</ol>
</div>

## Main takeaway

“Client ghosted after the proposal” isn’t a pause. It’s a race.

A race against competitors who write. A race against the client’s louder tasks. A race against their own “I’ll decide later.”

Your job isn’t “not to annoy”. It’s to stay useful: cases, answers, micro-steps.

Five touches over 21 days, in the right order: **24h — day 3 — day 7 — day 14 — day 21**.

Each touch has a reason. Each touch has a micro-step. No empty pings.

If after that the client doesn’t come back, they didn’t disappear. It’s real “not now”. That’s fine.

What matters is you closed the loop cleanly, with no bitterness, so you can come back in 3 months without awkwardness.
