Last May I had a meeting with a client. Great consulting fit, everything matched. At the end he said: “Really interesting. Let me think about it.” I answered: “Of course, no pressure.” Then he disappeared for 4 months.

Four months later he wrote: “We went with someone else. They were cheaper and faster to respond.”

That’s when it clicked: **“Let me think” isn’t a pause. It’s a signal that the client is missing something right now.** And if you don’t pull that “something” out in the first 30 seconds, the client will go think alone. Which means: with a competitor who had the courage to ask.

<div class="callout is-key">
<p class="callout-title">⚡️ Key idea</p>
<p>“Let me think about it” means “I’m missing something to decide.” Your job is to find what it is. Not to “give time”, but to <strong>ask one good question</strong> now.</p>
</div>

## Why “let me think” isn’t a rejection

In the Golden Key of Sales method, an objection is not “no”, it’s a **barrier**.

“Let me think” is the most common and the most dangerous barrier because it’s soft. It sounds polite. It doesn’t trigger you to push back. You both leave thinking “all good”.

In reality, “let me think” usually means one of five things:

<ul class="check">
<li><strong>They doubt the value</strong> — they don’t understand what they’re paying for</li>
<li><strong>They’re afraid to take the risk</strong> — what if it doesn’t work, they lose money/time</li>
<li><strong>They need approval</strong> — there’s another decision-maker: partner, director, finance</li>
<li><strong>It’s not a priority right now</strong> — budget/time is going elsewhere</li>
<li><strong>They’re comparing</strong> — they’re talking to competitors in parallel</li>
</ul>

Five reasons. Five different response strategies.

If you go in with a pre-made “but you see the ROI…” you miss 4 times out of 5.

<div class="callout is-tip">
<p class="callout-title">💡 Rule #1</p>
<p>Never answer “let me think” with arguments until the client says what exactly they’ll be thinking about. Question first, then answer. Otherwise you’re talking into a void.</p>
</div>

## What NOT to say

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“Of course, take your time! If you have questions, message me anytime.”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? You just **gave the client permission to disappear**.

No next step. No date. No hook. They’ll message you with the same probability they open an email from an unknown bank. Which is: never.

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“Come on, what’s there to think about? The price is fair, we have case studies, let’s start!”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? Pure **pressure**.

The client hears: “He needs me to pay, not to understand.” Defensive response: “I’ll think about it.”

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“What exactly is bothering you? Price? Timing? Maybe I can move a bit?”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? You **offered a discount before hearing the objection**.

They didn’t even say “too expensive” — and you already signaled the price is negotiable.

<div class="callout is-quote">
<p class="callout-title">Quote</p>
<p>“When a client says ‘I’ll think about it’, what they really mean is ‘I don’t have an answer to one internal question.’ Your job is to find the question without asking it directly.”</p>
<cite>— Michael Bang, lesson #15 "Objection Handling"</cite>
</div>

## Step 1. Don’t argue. Slow down.

You hear “let me think” — pause 2-3 seconds.

Don’t rush. Calmness in your voice right now is worth more than any argument.

Then use a permission line:

<div class="phrase-card">“Got it. Can I ask a personal question…?”</div>

“Personal question” is a magic wrapper. It lowers defenses. It sounds like a request, not a push.

## Step 2. Pull out the real reason

Your goal is to identify which of the five reasons is behind “let me think”. Use questions like:

<div class="phrase-card">“What exactly do you want to think through: the value, the terms, the risks, or do you need to run it by someone?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Is this ‘let me think’ more about a doubt (something unclear) or about timing (not now)?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“If we imagine you already decided — what could go wrong?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Who besides you needs to say yes? Does it make sense to discuss it with them now?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“When do you plan to come back to this decision — this week, this month, or next quarter?”</div>

<div class="callout is-tip">
<p class="callout-title">💡 Bridge line</p>
<p>Once they name a reason, close it with one readiness-check sentence: “If we clarify this right now, would you be ready to move forward?” If they say yes, you give the info. If they say no, the named reason wasn’t the real one.</p>
</div>

## Step 3. Answer precisely by scenario

### Scenario A: Value doubt or fear of risk

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Script:</strong> “Okay, thanks for being honest. Can I address that in 30 seconds? For your goal [one sentence], we do [mechanism], and we check the result by [specific metric in 30/60/90 days]. If the number doesn’t move, we adjust the approach. This isn’t ‘buy and pray’ — it’s partnered work with a clear checkpoint.”</p>
</div>

The key move: give a success metric and a checkpoint. It removes the fear of “what if it doesn’t work”.

### Scenario B: They need approval (partner/boss)

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Script:</strong> “Got it. Let’s not waste your week retelling this. What’s critical for that person: price, timelines, risk, comparison with alternatives? I can either write you a short forwardable text in 5 minutes, or we can do a 15-minute 3-way call. What’s easier?”</p>
</div>

<div class="phrase-card">[Name], we’re discussing [topic]. Goal: [outcome]. Format: [how]. Key risks to know: [1-2 points]. If you’re open, let’s do a 10-15 minute call.</div>

### Scenario C: Not a priority / not now

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Script:</strong> “Okay. Let’s be direct — is this ‘let me think’ about timing, or is this topic just not a priority right now? No hard feelings either way.”</p>
</div>

If they say “not a priority”, don’t push. Lock a next step and let them go:

<div class="phrase-card">“Okay, got it. I’ll message you on [specific date in 1-3 months]: two short lines on what’s new. If it’s relevant, we’ll book 10 minutes. If not, just say ‘not now’ and I won’t chase.”</div>

Roughly 25-30% of these come back in 2-6 months ready to buy.

## Real dialog

€9,500 deal for a marketing audit. Client: founder of an e-commerce brand.

<div class="dialog">
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">I really liked this, but I need to think about it.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">Got it. Can I ask a personal question: what exactly do you want to think through — the value, the terms, the risks, or do you need approval?</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">I’m not sure we’re internally ready. Small team. I’m not sure they can handle the process.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">That’s a real concern. If I show you how we do this without overloading your team — would you be ready to move forward?</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">If it’s truly without overload — yes.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">Okay. Short version: first 2 weeks it’s only me working with your data, no work for your team. Week 3: one 30-minute session with your marketer. After that: a weekly email report. That’s less load than an average internal project. Want me to send a one-pager with the task split?</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">Yes, perfect. And let’s talk on Wednesday to align on a start.</span></p>
</div>

The deal closed on Wednesday. No pressure, no discount.

## 7 ready-to-use lines

<div class="phrase-card">“Got it. Can I ask a personal question — what exactly do you want to think through?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Is this ‘let me think’ about timing or about a doubt?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“If we imagine you already decided — what could go wrong?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“If we clarify this right now, would you be ready to move forward?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Who besides you needs to say yes? Does it make sense to do a quick 3-way call?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Okay, let’s lock a date — I’ll message you on [day], two short lines.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“No hard feelings either way. If it’s simply not a priority right now — just say so.”</div>

## Checklist before you hear “let me think”

<ul class="check">
<li>Did I clarify who actually decides?</li>
<li>Did I restate the client’s goal in their words (not mine)?</li>
<li>Do I have one short real example (not made-up stats)?</li>
<li>Can I explain the outcome + checkpoint in 30 seconds?</li>
<li>Do we have a next step ready (date, format, participants)?</li>
</ul>

## Common mistakes

<div class="callout is-warn">
<p class="callout-title">⚠️ What kills the deal right now</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Arguing immediately</strong> without clarifying what they’re thinking about.</li>
<li><strong>Leaving without a next step.</strong> “Sure, think about it” = the client disappears.</li>
<li><strong>Begging for a decision right now.</strong> “Come on, what’s there to think about?” ends the deal.</li>
<li><strong>Confusing “let me think” with <a href="{{ site.baseurl }}/en/guides/objection-its-too-expensive/">“too expensive”</a>.</strong> Different reasons, different strategy.</li>
<li><strong>Pinging every 2 days.</strong> The 3rd reminder feels like stalking.</li>
</ol>
</div>

## Main takeaway

“Let me think” isn’t a pause. It’s a **hidden objection that has no name** until you pull it out.

Your tool is not an argument. It’s a question.

One good question saves weeks of back-and-forth and keeps deals alive that would otherwise quietly die.

After “let me think”, never leave without a locked next step: a date, a format, and participants.
