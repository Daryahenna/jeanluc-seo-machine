Cold call.

You start: “Hi, my name is [name], I’m from…” and you hear: “I don’t have time.”

In the first 5 seconds you have a choice:

Keep running your memorized script (“It’ll only take a minute!”) and lose the call in the next 10 seconds.

Or say one line that often flips the conversation.

I learned the second option after my average cold call lasted 8 seconds. Now it’s 2 minutes, and every third meeting turns into a second conversation.

The difference isn’t the product.

The difference is **what you say when you hear “no time.”**

<div class="callout is-key">
<p class="callout-title">⚡️ Key idea</p>
<p>“No time” means “I don’t understand why I should care” in ~80% of cases. The fix isn’t begging for a minute. It’s <strong>showing value in 10 seconds</strong>. If you do, time appears.</p>
</div>

## Why “no time” is a reflex, not a rejection

Golden Key observation: the first reaction to a cold contact is defense.

The client’s brain doesn’t analyze what you’re saying. It just blocks the attack.

“No time” is a standard defense mantra.

In reality, “no time” usually means one of four things:

<ul class="check">
<li><strong>They’re genuinely busy right now</strong> — driving, in a meeting, mid-task</li>
<li><strong>Defense reflex</strong> — their brain blocks any stranger automatically</li>
<li><strong>Not interested, but polite</strong> — real “no” wrapped softly</li>
<li><strong>They didn’t understand why you called</strong> — your opening was weak</li>
</ul>

Four reasons. Four different responses.

If you go with the classic “it’ll only take a minute”, you’ve already admitted you’re taking a valuable resource. Now they just want it to end.

<div class="callout is-tip">
<p class="callout-title">💡 Rule #1</p>
<p>Never apologize for your call. “Sorry to bother you”, “just one minute”, “I’ll be quick” puts you in the position of “I’m interrupting you”. You’re not. You’re bringing a potentially useful offer. Talk as an equal, not a beggar.</p>
</div>

## What NOT to say

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“I know you’re busy, but it’ll just take one little minute!”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? You **confirmed you’re interrupting**.

And “little minute” (diminutive) annoys any busy person. Defensive response: “I don’t even have a minute. Bye.”

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“When would be a good time to call you back?”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? You’re **asking for time without saying why**.

They have no reason to give you a calendar slot because you didn’t say what you’re offering. Most common answer: “I’ll reach out.” Then silence.

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“Sorry for bothering you, I caught you at a bad time…”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? You **devalued your call** in the first 3 seconds.

In their head, the call is over. Anything you say after is background noise.

<div class="callout is-quote">
<p class="callout-title">Quote</p>
<p>“A cold call isn’t an interruption. It’s an offer. If the offer is valuable, people always have 30 seconds to listen. If they don’t hear value, no ‘just a minute’ will save you.”</p>
<cite>— Michael Bang, lesson #11 "Prospecting"</cite>
</div>

## Step 1. Acknowledge it without apologizing

You hear “no time”. Calm. No rush. No excuses:

<div class="phrase-card">“Got it. I’m calling about [one specific thing]. I’ll take 20 seconds, then you decide — do we continue or not?”</div>

This works because:
1. You don’t ask for time — you set the frame (20 seconds)
2. You give control to the client (“you decide”)
3. You name one concrete topic

## Step 2. Deliver value in 20 seconds

This is the most important cold call skill.

You get 20 seconds to say **one** valuable thing. Not three. Not five. One.

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Structure (20 seconds):</strong></p>
<p>1. <strong>Who you help</strong>: “We work with [business type] like yours…”</p>
<p>2. <strong>The specific problem</strong>: “…who struggle with [specific pain].”</p>
<p>3. <strong>The outcome</strong>: “In 90 days they get [specific number/fact].”</p>
<p>4. <strong>Hook question</strong>: “If that’s relevant, I can show how in 10 minutes this week. If not, we end now. Which is better?”</p>
</div>

When you’re specific, the client can decide fast.

That’s 10x faster than “Let me tell you more.”

## Step 3. Close the conversation cleanly

After your hook question, there are three paths. Here are ready responses:

### Option A: “Yes, relevant, let’s do it this week”

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Script:</strong> “Great. I’m free Tuesday at 3pm or Wednesday at 11am. What works better?”</p>
</div>

Two concrete slots reduce effort. They pick one, you send the invite within an hour.

### Option B: “Tell me more right now”

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Script:</strong> “Okay. I have 5 minutes right now — exactly enough to see if it’s worth continuing. Quick question: what’s your current situation with [their topic]?”</p>
</div>

You flip the call from your pitch to their story. That qualifies faster.

### Option C: “No, not interested”

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Script:</strong> “Understood. Thanks for being direct. If something changes this year, keep my contact. Have a good day.”</p>
</div>

Don’t beg. Don’t force a close. A clear “no” is a gift.

## Real dialog

Cold call. Demo booked in 90 seconds. Client: head of marketing at a B2B SaaS.

<div class="dialog">
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">Hi Michael! My name is [name], I’m from [company]. I’m calling about one specific thing. I’ll take 20 seconds, then you decide if we continue.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">Sorry, I don’t have time right now.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">Got it. 20 seconds: we work with B2B SaaS marketing leaders like you. We help increase MQL-to-SQL conversion — typically from 12% to 22% in 60 days. If that’s relevant, I can show how in 10 minutes this week. If not, we end now. Which is better?</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">Hmm, this is actually painful. Let’s do it this week.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">Great. Thursday 11am or Friday 2pm — what works better?</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">Thursday, 11am.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">Perfect. I’ll send a Google Meet link and a one-pager on what we’ll cover so you come prepared. Talk Thursday.</span></p>
</div>

Call time: 90 seconds. Demo booked. No pressure. No “just a minute”.

## 6 ready-to-use lines

<div class="phrase-card">“Got it. I’ll take 20 seconds, then you decide — do we continue or not.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“I’m calling about one specific thing: [one sentence]. Is that relevant for you or not?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“We work with [segment], help with [pain]. Outcome: [number/fact].”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“If it’s relevant — 10 minutes this week. If not, we end now. Which is better?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Tuesday at 3pm or Wednesday at 11am — what works better?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Understood. Thanks for being direct. If something changes this year — keep my contact.”</div>

## Cold call checklist

<ul class="check">
<li>Can I explain who I help, what pain I solve, and what outcome I deliver in 20 seconds?</li>
<li>Do I have 2-3 specific meeting slots ready?</li>
<li>Am I avoiding “sorry”, “just a minute”, “I’m interrupting”?</li>
<li>Am I ready to hear “no” calmly and close cleanly?</li>
<li>Do I have a short follow-up text if they hang up?</li>
</ul>

## Common mistakes

<div class="callout is-warn">
<p class="callout-title">⚠️ What kills cold calls</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>“It’ll only take a minute.”</strong> The most annoying line. Guaranteed hang-up.</li>
<li><strong>Apologizing for calling.</strong> “Sorry to bother you” = I’m bothering you = we both want this to end.</li>
<li><strong>Asking for time without value.</strong> “When’s a good time?” without a value line = silence.</li>
<li><strong>A long memorized script.</strong> The client hears it in 5 seconds and checks out.</li>
<li><strong>Not letting go after “no”.</strong> The third “let me explain” gets you marked as spam.</li>
</ol>
</div>

## Main takeaway

“No time” is a **value test**.

If you can show value in 20 seconds, time appears. If you can’t, no “just a minute” will save you.

Cold calls aren’t violations. They’re offers.

Talk as an equal, not a beggar.

And remember: a clear “no” is better than a month of vague “maybe”.
