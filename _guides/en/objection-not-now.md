One of my early clients said: “Let’s come back to this next quarter.” I answered: “Of course, good luck!” and set myself a reminder for three months.

Three months later I wrote. He replied: “Thanks, we’re already working with someone else. You should’ve moved faster, I got tired of waiting.”

Since then I remember: **“later” isn’t “later”. It’s “now, but not with you.”**

If a client is willing to postpone for a quarter, they’re also willing to solve the problem in a week with the person who shows them the path.

<div class="callout is-key">
<p class="callout-title">⚡️ Key idea</p>
<p>“Let’s revisit later” means “I don’t see how to fit this into my life right now.” Your job is <strong>not to leave quietly</strong>, but to help the client see the exact moment when it becomes logical again.</p>
</div>

## Why “later” isn’t about time

Golden Key logic is simple: when a client says “not now”, either they don’t clearly see the outcome yet, or there’s an external factor (budget, seasonality, an event). Less often, it’s truly not a priority.

In reality, “later” usually means one of three things:

<ul class="check">
<li><strong>It’s tied to a real event</strong> — waiting for quarter results, product launch, hiring, investor</li>
<li><strong>They’re overloaded right now</strong> — interested, but no bandwidth to start</li>
<li><strong>It’s not a priority in their head</strong> — sounds polite, but the problem doesn’t hurt</li>
</ul>

Three reasons. Three different responses.

If you don’t find which one it is, you lose the client into the black hole of “I’ll remind them sometime”.

<div class="callout is-tip">
<p class="callout-title">💡 Rule #1</p>
<p>Never leave without a specific next-step date. “Whenever works for you” = never. “May 20, I’ll send one message” = a real date in a calendar. That’s the difference between a deal next quarter and silence forever.</p>
</div>

## What NOT to say

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“Sure, no pressure! Reach out when it’s convenient.”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? You **moved initiative to the client**.

They won’t reach out. Not because you’re bad, but because they have 50 other things. Without a date from you, it’s over.

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“What exactly is distracting you right now? Maybe I can adapt to your schedule?”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? It sounds like you’re **trying to squeeze yourself into their life**.

Defensive response: “I’ll get back to you.” Then silence.

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“We have a promo right now, 15% off until the end of the month!”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? Fake urgency. Clients smell it. Trust drops to zero.

<div class="callout is-quote">
<p class="callout-title">Quote</p>
<p>“Delay isn’t ‘no’. It’s the client asking you for a frame so a decision becomes possible. Give them the frame, and the decision will follow.”</p>
<cite>— Michael Bang, lesson #15 "Objection Handling"</cite>
</div>

## Step 1. Accept it without pressure

You hear “later” — calm tone:

<div class="phrase-card">“Got it, thanks for saying that. So I know how to handle it…”</div>

Then immediately go into Step 2.

Don’t leave a pause where the client can end the conversation.

## Step 2. Pull out the reason behind “later”

One sharp question beats three vague ones:

<div class="phrase-card">“Is this ‘later’ tied to a specific event (launch, budget, season), or is it simply not the right time?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“If we remove ‘now’ — what needs to change for this to become relevant?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“What should we anchor on: a date, an event, budget availability?”</div>

<div class="callout is-tip">
<p class="callout-title">💡 Tiny word that changes everything</p>
<p>The phrase “tied to” is magic. It turns abstract “sometime” into a concrete trigger. Clients start naming events: “after Q2”, “when we hire two more”, “after the product release”. That’s your hook.</p>
</div>

## Step 3. Lock it in (in a way that helps the client)

This is critical: the date isn’t just for you. It helps the client too. Explain why.

### Scenario A: It’s tied to an event

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Script:</strong> “Okay, makes sense. Then let’s do this: I’ll send you one short message [a week after the event] — two lines on what we can do in the new situation. If it’s relevant, we’ll book 15 minutes. If not, just tell me and I won’t chase. Works?”</p>
</div>

### Scenario B: They’re overloaded

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Script:</strong> “Got it. Quick question: if this took only 30 minutes of your time per week, would it fit? I can design a format for your real schedule, not the other way around. Want me to suggest options?”</p>
</div>

Often “no time” is really fear that the project will eat their week. When you show a concrete “30 minutes”, interest comes back.

### Scenario C: It’s not a priority

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Script:</strong> “Okay, no hard feelings — it sounds like ‘not now, and not sure it ever will be’. That’s fine. If you want, I’ll send one useful resource on [their topic] — no CTA, no follow-ups. If it ever becomes relevant this year, you’ll message me. Deal?”</p>
</div>

This is a strong move. You say what people usually hide. Pressure drops to zero. About 30% come back in 4-9 months.

## Real dialog

€6,000 deal for quarterly consulting. Client: B2B SaaS founder, post-demo.

<div class="dialog">
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">I like it, but let’s come back to this in August.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">Got it. Is “August” tied to something specific — budget, hiring, a release?</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">Product release in July. Right now it’s just not the time.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">Makes sense. Then let’s do this: I’ll send you one message on August 5 — a short plan for how we can plug into the first 60 days after release without pulling you away during launch. If it clicks, we book. If not, you forget me. Fair?</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">Okay, let’s do it.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">And one last thing: if a question comes up in your team on [their area], message me informally and I’ll answer in 10 minutes. Free.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">Love it. Putting it in my calendar.</span></p>
</div>

On August 5 I wrote. On August 12 we signed.

## 6 ready-to-use lines

<div class="phrase-card">“Is this ‘later’ tied to an event, or is it simply not the right time?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“What should we anchor on: a date, an event, budget availability?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“What needs to change for this to become relevant?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“I’ll message you on [specific date] — one message, two lines. If it clicks, we book.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“If this took 30 minutes a week — would it fit your schedule?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“If a question comes up, message me — I’ll answer in 10 minutes. Free.”</div>

## Checklist before you lock the next step

<ul class="check">
<li>Did I learn whether “later” is tied to an event or it’s “not a priority”?</li>
<li>Did I lock a specific date (not “sometime”)?</li>
<li>Did I explain why the date helps them (not just me)?</li>
<li>Did I give value right now (a useful fact, resource, help)?</li>
<li>Did I avoid fake urgency (“promo ends this week”)?</li>
</ul>

## Common mistakes

<div class="callout is-warn">
<p class="callout-title">⚠️ What kills the deal</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Leaving without a date.</strong> “Reach out when it’s convenient” = silence.</li>
<li><strong>Fake urgency.</strong> Clients feel it instantly and trust drops.</li>
<li><strong>Too frequent pings.</strong> Weekly = chasing. Monthly + relevant = rhythm.</li>
<li><strong>Confusing “later” with <a href="{{ site.baseurl }}/en/guides/objection-let-me-think/">“let me think”</a>.</strong> Different reasons, different strategy.</li>
<li><strong>Providing zero value before the return date.</strong> In 3 months they’ll forget you.</li>
</ol>
</div>

## Main takeaway

“Let’s revisit later” is a **request for a frame**.

They’re not rejecting you. They’re asking you to help them see the exact moment when it becomes relevant.

Give them that moment as a real date tied to their reality. And don’t disappear between now and then.

The most expensive phrase in sales isn’t “no”. It’s “I’ll message you sometime.” Never leave it in a conversation.
