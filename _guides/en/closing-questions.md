Last May I was closing a €25,000 deal with a founder. Everything went perfectly: qualification, presentation, objections.

At the end I used a memorized line: “So… do we sign the contract?”

The client’s face changed. He said: “Let me think about it.” Two weeks later the deal went to a competitor for the same price.

For a long time I couldn’t understand what happened.

Then I reviewed the call with my coach. He stopped me on one word: “contract”.

He said: “In one second you turned a live conversation into a legal procedure.”

Since then I don’t use that word at all. My close rate went up by 18 percentage points.

<div class="callout is-key">
<p class="callout-title">⚡️ Key idea</p>
<p>Closing isn’t forcing. It’s <strong>a readiness check + handing initiative to the client</strong>. If you do it right, the client says “let’s get started” themselves. If you push, even a good-fit deal breaks at the last minute.</p>
</div>

## Why “hard closing” doesn’t work anymore

Golden Key logic is simple: if the first 5 steps of the sale (prospecting, relationship building, qualifying, presentation, objection handling) are done well, closing happens **naturally**.

If you start “pushing” at step 6, it means one of the first five steps was weak.

Modern closing isn’t one final line. It’s three types of questions in sequence:

<ul class="check">
<li><strong>Ready-questions</strong> — check that the client is ready to decide</li>
<li><strong>Choice-questions</strong> — a soft choice between two acceptable options</li>
<li><strong>Closing phrase</strong> — one short line that moves into action</li>
</ul>

If you skip the first two and jump to the third, you create pressure. The client feels it and defends. The deal breaks.

<div class="callout is-tip">
<p class="callout-title">💡 Rule #1</p>
<p>Never use a closing phrase if you’re not sure the client already said “yes” in their head. Start with a ready-question and a choice-question. Only if both are green lights do you move to the final line.</p>
</div>

## What NOT to say when closing

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“So… do we sign the contract?”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? **The word “contract” turns on the client’s inner lawyer.**

In one second, a human conversation becomes a formal process with signatures and liability. Defensive response: “Let me think again.”

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“This price is only valid until the end of the week. If you don’t decide now, it’ll be more expensive on Monday!”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? **Fake deadline**.

Clients feel it, especially if you never mentioned any increase before. Trust drops. The perceived value drops too.

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“I know you’re unsure, but honestly this is the best offer you’ll find. Let’s sign already.”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? **Pressure + self-praise**.

Clients think: “If it’s truly that good, why are you pushing so hard?” Good offers don’t need “let’s sign already”.

<div class="callout is-quote">
<p class="callout-title">Quote</p>
<p>“Closing is the most delicate part of a sale. If you did the first five steps well, closing is just two or three good questions. If you didn’t, no closing technique will save you.”</p>
<cite>— Michael Bang, lesson #16 "Closing"</cite>
</div>

## Step 1. Ready-questions

**Goal:** make sure there are no unspoken questions.

These are open, non-pushy questions:

<div class="phrase-card">“Do you have any questions I haven’t answered?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Do you have everything you need to make a decision?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Does this fit what you were looking for?”</div>

If they answer “yes, all good”, go to step 2.

If they say “I have a question about X”, answer it fully and calmly, then return to the ready-question.

If you ignore the last unspoken question, the client takes it home and says “let me think”. I’ve seen dozens of deals die that way.

<div class="callout is-tip">
<p class="callout-title">💡 Pause after the ready-question</p>
<p>After “Do you have everything you need?” go silent. 5-10 seconds. Silence isn’t scary, it’s the work. Many sellers fill it with chatter and break the moment. Let the client think. In half the cases they’ll say the last doubt they otherwise would’ve kept inside.</p>
</div>

## Step 2. Choice-questions

**Goal:** move the conversation from “buy / don’t buy” to “what’s the best way”.

It removes the feeling of a big irreversible act. They’re just choosing between two options, and the “yes” is implied.

Examples:

<div class="phrase-card">“Would you rather start Monday or Wednesday?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Is the annual package or the quarterly package a better fit?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Do we pay by card or via invoice from the company?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Do we start with the product or with team training?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Should we set this up under you personally or under your company?”</div>

Three properties of a good choice-question:

<ul class="check">
<li><strong>Both options are acceptable to you.</strong> If one option is bad, don’t offer it.</li>
<li><strong>Both options imply yes.</strong> Not “start now or think for a week”. That’s pressure.</li>
<li><strong>The options are concrete.</strong> Not “whenever”, but “May 6 or May 13”.</li>
</ul>

What happens next?

**Option A.** They pick one. That’s a “yes”. Go to step 3.

**Option B.** They hesitate or say “I’m not sure yet”. That means something earlier is missing.

Don’t push. Go one step back: “Got it. What’s blocking a decision right now — one specific thing, or a general feeling?”

Then work it with ["Let me think about it"]({{ site.baseurl }}/en/guides/objection-let-me-think/) or ["It's too expensive"]({{ site.baseurl }}/en/guides/objection-its-too-expensive/).

## Step 3. Closing phrase (without “contract”)

**Goal:** turn the “yes” in the client’s head into action.

Your closing phrase should be:

<ul class="check">
<li>Short (one sentence)</li>
<li>Action-based (“I’ll send”, “we’ll set up”, “we’ll book”)</li>
<li>Without the word “contract” (use “paperwork”, “agreement”, “let’s get started”)</li>
<li>Natural tone (not pleading, not pushy)</li>
</ul>

Working options:

<div class="phrase-card">“Okay. Let’s get the paperwork done.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Then I’ll send the agreement tonight.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Great. We’re starting. I’ll prep the first step.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Booked. I’ll send the details within the hour.”</div>

After the closing phrase: pause.

Let the client confirm with a nod, a “yes”, or even silence.

Don’t fill the gap with “we can always change later”. That signals “I’m not sure you’re sure”, and the doubts come back.

<div class="callout is-tip">
<p class="callout-title">💡 Replacing the word “contract”</p>
<p>In my calls, the word “contract” increased last-minute resistance by ~30%. Swapping it for “paperwork” / “let’s get started” / “agreement” brought the close rate back. In B2B you often do need a contract, but “I’ll prepare the documents” is 10x softer than “Do we sign the contract?” Words have physical impact on decisions.</p>
</div>

## Real dialog

€15,000 deal for quarterly strategy consulting.

Client: operations director at a B2B company, after 3 meetings and 2 clarification calls.

<div class="dialog">
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">Okay, we went through the whole proposal. Before we move — do you have any questions I haven’t answered?</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">Hmm… not really. It’s pretty clear.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">Do you have everything you need to make a decision?</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">Yes, I think so.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">Then one question: would you rather start Monday, May 6, or Monday, May 13?</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">May 13. I need a week to pull the team out of the current campaign.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">Agreed. May 13. I’ll prepare onboarding and send the schedule tonight.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">Great. Waiting for it.</span></p>
</div>

Deal closed. No “contract”. No pressure. No discount.

## 8 ready-to-use closing lines

Use them in this exact order: ready → choice → close.

<div class="phrase-card">“Do you have any questions I haven’t answered?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Do you have everything you need to make a decision?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Does this fit what you were looking for?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Would you rather start on [date A] or [date B]?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Is the annual package or the quarterly package a better fit?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Do we pay by card or via invoice?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Okay. Let’s get the paperwork done.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Great. We’re starting. I’ll prep the first step.”</div>

## Checklist before closing

30 seconds before you ask a ready-question:

<ul class="check">
<li>Did I address every objection the client voiced?</li>
<li>Do I have 2 concrete options for a choice-question (dates, formats, payment methods)?</li>
<li>Do I know my closing phrase (short, no “contract”)?</li>
<li>Am I comfortable with 5-10 seconds of silence after a ready-question?</li>
<li>Will I avoid filling the silence with excuses or discounts?</li>
</ul>

If you have even one “no”, you’re closing too early.

## Common mistakes

<div class="callout is-warn">
<p class="callout-title">⚠️ What kills the deal at the closing moment</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Jumping into a closing phrase</strong> without a ready-question.</li>
<li><strong>The word “contract”.</strong> Minus ~30% conversion. Replace it.</li>
<li><strong>“So… are we signing?”</strong> Turns a human conversation into a legal procedure.</li>
<li><strong>Filling the silence.</strong> 5-10 seconds after the closing line is correct. Don’t apologize, discount, or change topics.</li>
<li><strong>Fake urgency.</strong> “Only until Friday” works once and poisons trust.</li>
<li><strong>Unequal options in a choice-question.</strong> “Start now or think for a week?” is pressure dressed up as choice.</li>
</ol>
</div>

## Main takeaway

Closing isn’t the final battle. It’s a readiness check.

If the first 5 steps were done well, closing happens through a simple 3-part protocol.

**Ready-questions** check what’s unspoken.

**Choice-questions** move from “buy?” to “what’s the best way?”.

**Closing phrase** turns “yes” into action — with one short line, without the word “contract”.

The most common mistake is skipping the first two and jumping to the third.

Add two questions before your final line and your close rate typically rises 15-25 points.
