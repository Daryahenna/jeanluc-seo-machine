If you've been selling for more than a year, you have a personal counter: how many times a month you hear “too expensive”. My first year it was 8 out of 10. For clients in training it's usually 6 out of 10. For strong sellers it's 2-3.

The difference isn't the product. And it isn't the price.

The difference is **what they do in the first 4 seconds after that word.**

Those 4 seconds decide whether the deal survives. And that's exactly where most people mess it up.

<div class="callout is-key">
<p class="callout-title">⚡️ Key idea</p>
<p>“Too expensive” isn’t a rejection. It’s a <strong>signal that something is missing</strong>. Your job is to find what’s missing, not to prove your price is fair.</p>
</div>

## Why “too expensive” almost never means “too expensive”

In the Golden Key of Sales method (the one I teach), there’s a simple rule: **an objection isn’t “no”, it’s a barrier.**

If the client says “too expensive”, there’s already interest. Otherwise they’d just leave.

In reality, “too expensive” usually means one of five things:

<ul class="check">
<li><strong>They truly don’t have the money</strong> — the budget isn’t there; they like it, but can’t pay</li>
<li><strong>They found a cheaper alternative</strong> — they’re comparing a different scope, format, or competitor</li>
<li><strong>They don’t see the value</strong> — they don’t understand what they’re paying for and what result they’ll get</li>
<li><strong>They’re scared to take the risk</strong> — what if it doesn’t work, they lose money/time</li>
<li><strong>They want a different payment structure</strong> — price is OK, but they need installments/milestones/another package</li>
</ul>

Five reasons. Five different answers.

If you answer without knowing the reason, you miss 4 times out of 5. Which leads to Rule #1:

<div class="callout is-tip">
<p class="callout-title">💡 Rule #1</p>
<p>Never answer “too expensive” right away. First clarify what exactly is “expensive”. That’s Step 2 in Bang’s method. Without it, any line you use will likely miss.</p>
</div>

## What NOT to say (and why)

Before I give you the lines that work, here’s what most people do and why they lose the deal.

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“It’s not expensive if you calculate the value of the modules separately. It’s actually a great deal because you get A, and B, and C, and a bonus, and a consultation…”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? You’re **arguing**.

The client just gave you an opinion. If you argue, you automatically make them “wrong”. Nobody likes feeling wrong. Defensive response: “Whatever, it’s expensive. Bye.”

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“Okay, what if I give you a 15% discount?”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? You **devalued** your offer in one second.

Now the client thinks: “If they dropped 15% instantly, I can get another 15%.” Next: haggling, where you’re in the begging position.

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“I get it. Our price is higher than the market because our quality…”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? You just **agreed that it’s expensive**.

Now the client is 100% sure they’re overpaying. Anything you say about quality will sound like an excuse.

<div class="callout is-quote">
<p class="callout-title">Quote</p>
<p>“Explaining your price without understanding the objection is a monologue. Selling is a dialogue.”</p>
<cite>— Michael Bang, lesson #15 "Objection Handling"</cite>
</div>

## Step 1. Listen (4 seconds)

Sounds obvious, but 80% of sellers don’t do it.

They hear “too expensive” and immediately start pitching.

**What to do:** pause. Literally 4 seconds of silence.

That pause tells the client you’re taking them seriously, not throwing a canned rebuttal.

Then use a short echo line. No evaluation. No agreement. No argument:

<div class="phrase-card">Got it. Tell me…</div>

Pause again.

Let the client continue if they want. Sometimes after “Got it” they’ll give you the reason for free: “Well look, I’m self-employed and this quarter I already spent on…”

## Step 2. Clarify (the most important step)

Now you work.

Your goal is to identify which of the five reasons sits behind “too expensive” in one or two questions.

Pick what fits the context:

<div class="phrase-card">“When you say ‘too expensive’ — is it more about budget (no money) or value (not clear what you’re paying for)?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“If we remove the price for a minute — does this fit what you need?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“What outcome would make the price feel fair?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“What are you comparing it to? Let’s compare like for like: scope, timelines, accountability.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Who besides you looks at the budget? Does it make sense to loop them in now?”</div>

<div class="callout is-tip">
<p class="callout-title">💡 Messaging hack</p>
<p>If you’re in chat and the client answers with one-liners, give them a fork: two options to choose from. It’s much easier to reply “1” or “2” than to write a paragraph. Reply rate goes up 30-40%.</p>
</div>

## Step 3. Answer precisely

Only now, when you know the reason, you’ve earned the right to talk.

Below are four scenarios.

### Scenario A: “I don’t see the value”

The most common one.

The client doesn’t clearly understand what they’re buying and what result they’ll get.

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Script:</strong> “Okay. In 30 seconds: for your situation [one sentence in their words], we do [the mechanism], and we’ll know it’s working by [success metric]. That’s why the price isn’t for hours — it’s for an outcome. If you want, I can break it down: what’s included and how we track progress.”</p>
</div>

Three must-have elements:
1. **Tie it to their situation** — show you listened
2. **Mechanism** — how it works, no mystical stuff
3. **Success metric** — how you’ll both know it worked

### Scenario B: “There are cheaper options”

They’re comparing.

Your job isn’t to trash a competitor. It’s to move the conversation to: “Let’s compare the same thing.”

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Script:</strong> “Got it. What exactly are you comparing it to? I don’t want to guess. Let’s compare on four parameters: scope, timelines, accountability for the outcome, and risks. Then we’ll see if it’s the same service or a different format.”</p>
</div>

Often after this the client says it themselves: “Ah, right, they don’t include any guarantee.” Done.

### Scenario C: “I’m scared to take the risk”

Price isn’t the problem. Fear is.

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Script:</strong> “If we remove the price — what worries you more: that we won’t get the result, or that the process will be painful? That tells me what we need to clarify.”</p>
</div>

After that question, clients usually name the real fear. Then you can address it directly (guarantee, checkpoints, phased work, similar case studies).

### Scenario D: “Budget is truly tight”

Don’t “sell harder”. **Fit the format.**

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>Script:</strong> “Okay. My job isn’t to convince you — it’s to find a format that still moves you forward within your constraints. What range is realistic for you right now?”</p>
</div>

Then you offer a reduced scope, milestone payments, installments, or prioritization: “We do X in month one, and Y later once X produces results.”

## Real dialog (how it sounds live)

So you can hear how the three steps sound in a real conversation, here’s a real dialog with a client: an IT entrepreneur buying a yearly consulting package for €18,000.

<div class="dialog">
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">Look, it’s expensive. Eighteen thousand for a year is serious money.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">Got it. When you say “expensive” — is it more about budget or about value?</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">More like I don’t understand what I get for that kind of money.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">Okay. Then short version: for your goal — taking cold-call conversion from 8% to 20% — we work through three blocks over the year: scripts, role-play practice, and weekly reviews of your real calls. In 90 days we both see the number. If it doesn’t move, we change the approach. This isn’t “buy and forget” — it’s partnered work. The price is for me being with you for a year.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">How often are the reviews?</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">Every week for 30 minutes. Plus you can reach me in chat between reviews.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">Hmm. Alright, tell me more.</span></p>
</div>

The deal closed a week later. No discount.

## 8 ready-to-use lines

Save these and drill them until they’re automatic:

<div class="phrase-card">“Got it. And ‘too expensive’ — is it more about budget or value?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“If the price were fine — would this fit what you need?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“What are you comparing it to? Let’s compare the same parameters.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“What outcome would make the price feel fair?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“What worries you more: the result, or the risk of getting it wrong?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Can I show you what the price includes and where the outcome is for you?”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“If the budget is tight — let’s find a format that still moves you forward.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Who else is involved in the decision? Let’s loop them in now so we don’t lose time.”</div>

## Checklist before you open your mouth

When you hear “too expensive”, run this in 10 seconds:

<ul class="check">
<li>Do I understand the client’s goal and context?</li>
<li>Do I know who decides and what the budget range is?</li>
<li>Can I explain the outcome in one paragraph, without jargon?</li>
<li>Am I trying to argue — or trying to understand?</li>
<li>Am I avoiding a discount as my first move?</li>
</ul>

If you have even one “no” — you’re not ready to answer. Clarify first.

## Common mistakes I see every day

<div class="callout is-warn">
<p class="callout-title">⚠️ What kills the deal right now</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Arguing.</strong> “It’s not expensive” guarantees conflict.</li>
<li><strong>Defending yourself immediately.</strong> Long explanations without clarifying sound like self-protection.</li>
<li><strong>Closing with value when the problem is budget.</strong> If they truly don’t have money, you’re hammering them with a pitch.</li>
<li><strong>Dropping the price as your first move.</strong> You devalue your offer yourself.</li>
<li><strong>Mixing up “too expensive” and “not now”.</strong> Different scenarios, different strategies.</li>
</ol>
</div>

## If the objection is legit — what do you do?

Sometimes after you clarify, you learn: there really is no budget, or this person isn’t the decision-maker.

You can’t “beat” that with a line. It’s a real stop factor.

So don’t push. Agree on the next step:

<div class="phrase-card">“Okay, I get the situation. I’m not trying to force anything right now. When you have budget / the decision — message me and we’ll come back to it. In the meantime, I’ll send you a short PDF breakdown to review when you have a minute.”</div>

This works long-term.

Roughly 30% of these “no, but later” deals come back in 2-6 months ready to buy. The key is: don’t burn the relationship with pressure today.

## Main takeaway

“Too expensive” isn’t the end of the conversation. It’s the start of the real conversation.

**The best response to “too expensive” isn’t a magic phrase. It’s a short 3-step process: listen, clarify, answer precisely.**

Once you know what sits behind “too expensive”, the objection stops being a scary wall and becomes a normal task: identify the reason → provide what’s missing.
