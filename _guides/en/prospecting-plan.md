In my first year in sales I was chaotic.

I’d wake up thinking “I need clients today”, spend the morning scrolling LinkedIn, then switch to calls, then write emails at night.

End of the month: 200 scattered touches, 3 meetings, 0 closed deals.

My coach looked at my activity and said: “You don’t have prospecting. You have nervous searching.”

Then he gave me a frame that changed everything: **5 channels, a fixed number of actions per day, and a weekly metric.**

The next quarter: 47 meetings, 12 closed deals.

It wasn’t magic.

It was a plan.

<div class="callout is-key">
<p class="callout-title">⚡️ Key idea</p>
<p>Prospecting isn’t “finding clients”. It’s <strong>a system of daily actions</strong> that produces a predictable flow of qualified meetings. Without a system you’re in survival mode. With a system you’re in growth mode.</p>
</div>

## Why “finding clients” doesn’t work

Golden Key principle: prospecting is **step 1 of the 6-step sale**.

If step 1 fails, the other five don’t matter.

No prospects means no qualification. No qualification means nothing to present. The pipeline is empty.

Most sellers make the same mistake: prospecting is “when I have time”.

In reality that means “never”. Time for calm work doesn’t appear by itself.

A working system has three parts:

<ul class="check">
<li><strong>Ideal customer profile (ICP)</strong> — who you sell to, narrow and clear</li>
<li><strong>5 channels</strong> — diversified sources so you don’t depend on one</li>
<li><strong>Rhythm</strong> — a fixed number of actions every day, on the clock</li>
</ul>

Without these three, you’re in chaos.

<div class="callout is-tip">
<p class="callout-title">💡 Rule #1</p>
<p>Prospecting happens in the morning, before ops tasks begin. It’s the most productive part of the day. Give it to growth, not reactions. If you leave it for “after lunch”, you’ll almost never do it. This is tested across hundreds of sellers.</p>
</div>

## What NOT to do

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“Today I’ll just hang out on LinkedIn and see who to message.”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? **Chaos with no goal or metric.**

At the end of the day you “worked”, but you don’t know how much you did or what worked.

In a week you burn out.

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“I’ll try 10 channels at once to find the best one.”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? **You won’t master any of them.**

Each channel needs 2-4 weeks to understand how it works in your market.

If you keep switching, every channel stays weak and you conclude “sales doesn’t work”.

<div class="bad-example">
<p>“I’ll run ads first. Leads will come in by themselves.”</p>
</div>

What’s wrong? **Ads bring cold leads with zero trust.**

Conversion is 5-10x lower than warm touches through networking/referrals.

If you don’t have an ICP and a basic funnel, ads just burn money.

<div class="callout is-quote">
<p class="callout-title">Quote</p>
<p>“Sustainable sales start with prospecting you do every day, regardless of mood. Everything else is reaction. Prospecting is initiative.”</p>
<cite>— Michael Bang, lesson #11 "Prospecting"</cite>
</div>

## Step 1. Define your ICP

Your ICP is **not “everyone who can buy.”**

It’s a specific segment with pain, budget, and authority.

<div class="phrase-card">Answer “Who is my ICP?” in one line: “[business/role] at [size/stage] who spends [budget] to solve [specific pain].”</div>

Example: “Founder of a B2B SaaS at 1-5M ARR, spending €30k-€100k/year on marketing, struggling with low demo conversion.”

If you don’t have a one-liner, your prospecting will be inefficient.

Don’t skip this.

<div class="callout is-tip">
<p class="callout-title">💡 Shortcut</p>
<p>Take your 5 best clients from last year. Find the overlap: company size, industry, DM role, trigger event. That’s your ICP. Not invented, but real.</p>
</div>

## Step 2. Pick 5 channels

Not more and not less.

Fewer and you depend too much on one. More and you can’t actually learn them.

### Channel 1: Referrals (fastest)

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>What to do:</strong> once a month ask 3-5 current/past clients: “Who else in your network could benefit from this? Can you introduce us?”</p>
</div>

Meeting conversion: 60-80%. About 10x higher than any cold channel.

### Channel 2: Networking (medium speed, high quality)

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>What to do:</strong> 1 targeted event per week (conference, meetup, business breakfast). Goal: 5 new conversations + contact exchange. Don’t sell on the spot. Just connect and capture interest.</p>
</div>

After the event, within 24 hours send a short message: “Great meeting you. I remember you mentioned [their topic] — if you want, I can share a couple of ideas.”

### Channel 3: Cold calls (fastest volume, needs practice)

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>What to do:</strong> 25 cold calls/day, morning block of 90 minutes. Use the opener from the guide <a href="{{ site.baseurl }}/en/guides/objection-no-time/">"I don't have time"</a>. Track: pickup rate + hook rate.</p>
</div>

Conversion is low (2-5% to a meeting), but **volume covers it**.

It’s the most underrated channel because most people are scared.

### Channel 4: Cold outreach (LinkedIn, email)

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>What to do:</strong> 30 personalized messages/day. Not a template. Each message includes one concrete detail about them (company, post, news). Cadence: 3 touches, then stop.</p>
</div>

Reply rate: 8-15% if your personalization is good. Meetings: 3-5%.

### Channel 5: Content (slow, but creates inbound)

<div class="good-example">
<p><strong>What to do:</strong> 2 posts/week on LinkedIn (or wherever your ICP actually lives). Topics: real cases, mistake breakdowns, practical lessons. No “motivation”. No “5 tips to…” fluff.</p>
</div>

After 3-6 months inbound starts.

Slowest, but produces the warmest leads.

## Step 3. Lock your weekly rhythm

Each channel needs a concrete number of actions per day/week.

Below is a solid pace for one person.

<div class="callout is-key">
<p class="callout-title">📊 Daily baseline (solo)</p>
<ul class="check">
<li><strong>Calls:</strong> 25/day, 90-minute morning block</li>
<li><strong>Outreach messages:</strong> 30 personalized messages</li>
<li><strong>Networking:</strong> 1 event/week + follow-up within 24 hours</li>
<li><strong>Referrals:</strong> 5 asks/month = 1/week</li>
<li><strong>Content:</strong> 2 posts/week + 30 min/day commenting</li>
</ul>
</div>

That’s ~55-60 touches/day.

Sounds like a lot, but with a rhythm it’s easy.

## Real dialog

Conversation with my client, a solo B2B marketing consultant. He complained: “I don’t have leads.”

<div class="dialog">
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">I just don’t have leads. I do everything I can, but nobody comes.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">How many new touches do you do per day across all channels, total?</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">Hmm… well… I messaged 2-3 people on LinkedIn yesterday. And I posted once last week.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">3 touches/day isn’t “no leads”. It’s “no prospecting”. A normal solo baseline is 50-60. Want to define your ICP and distribute those 50-60 across 5 channels?</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">50/day sounds impossible.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line"><span class="who">Seller:</span><span class="what">90 minutes in the morning = 25 calls. Another 60 minutes = 30 messages. One event per week. That’s 4 hours/week for new touches. If you can’t find 4 hours/week, your problem isn’t leads. It’s priorities.</span></p>
<p class="dialog-line is-them"><span class="who">Client:</span><span class="what">Alright. I’ll try 90 days.</span></p>
</div>

After 90 days: 12 meetings, 4 closed deals. No ad budget.

## 6 rules to keep

<div class="phrase-card">“One ICP in one line. Without it, you’re selling to everyone.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“5 channels. Not 3, not 10. Master these 5 in 90 days.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“90-minute morning block for calls. No distractions.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“One targeted event per week. 5 new conversations is the minimum.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“One referral ask per week.”</div>

<div class="phrase-card">“Metric: touches per week. Not deals. Not revenue. Touches.”</div>

## Morning checklist

<ul class="check">
<li>Do I know who I’m calling today (name, company, trigger)?</li>
<li>Is my 90-minute morning prospecting block protected (no meetings, no chats)?</li>
<li>Do I have an opening script for cold calls?</li>
<li>Do I know what metric I’ll measure at the end of the week?</li>
<li>Am I doing a fixed number of touches, not “working”? </li>
</ul>

## Common mistakes

<div class="callout is-warn">
<p class="callout-title">⚠️ What kills prospecting</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Doing it “when there’s time”.</strong> There won’t be time. Block it in your calendar.</li>
<li><strong>Jumping between channels.</strong> Master 1-2, then add more.</li>
<li><strong>Measuring deals instead of activity.</strong> Deals are the outcome. Activity is what you control.</li>
<li><strong>Prospecting without an ICP.</strong> That’s chaos, not prospecting.</li>
<li><strong>Not tracking conversion by channel.</strong> In a month you won’t know what works.</li>
</ol>
</div>

## Main takeaway

Prospecting isn’t talent. It’s discipline.

5 channels + a clear rhythm + daily activity + a weekly metric.

That’s it.

The best sellers I know aren’t uniquely gifted. They just do it every day.

And after 90 days they have what “talented” people don’t: a steady flow of qualified meetings.
